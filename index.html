Case 01 · Backlog Ownership

Chaotic backlog. Renewal risk. 90 days. Where do I start?

The situation: Inherited an unstructured backlog from four teams — no prioritization, no clear ownership. Customers are flagging data quality and usability issues that are beginning to threaten renewals. Leadership expects delivery on global priorities within 90 days. Engineering capacity is limited and partially committed.
Renewal Risk Limited Eng Capacity 90-Day Window Unstructured Backlog
Case 01 · Framework

Triage first. Structure second. Ship third.

Step 1 · Categorize everything
🔴 Revenue Risk
Data quality & usability tied to renewals
🌐 Global Mandates
Leadership-committed roadmap items
💡 Improvements
Nice-to-have, lower urgency
🗑 Noise
Duplicate, stale, or out-of-scope
Step 2 · Score each item
  • →
    Customer impact
    How many customers affected? Is it blocking a renewal conversation?
  • →
    Engineering effort
    Quick fix vs. multi-sprint. Map to available capacity — not wishlist.
  • →
    Strategic alignment
    Overlap with Global priorities? Double-count the value.
  • →
    Testability
    Can we define a clear acceptance criterion before writing a line of code?
Assumption

Engineering has at least 20–30% uncommitted capacity. I'd validate this on day one before confirming any commitments to leadership or CS.

Case 01 · Execution

90 days. Three phases. Two parallel tracks.

Days 1–14 · Triage
Orient & Diagnose
  • Audit backlog with CS, Sales & Eng
  • Tag renewal-risk items by customer impact
  • Confirm Eng capacity & committed work
  • Align on Global mandate scope
  • Create one shared backlog view
Days 15–45 · Deliver
Quick Wins + Foundations
  • Ship high-impact / low-effort fixes
  • Write testable requirements for Global items
  • Weekly sync: Eng, CS, Product
  • Communicate progress to at-risk customers
  • Lock definition of done with Eng
Days 46–90 · Ship
Global Priorities + Validate
  • Deliver committed Global roadmap items
  • Close renewal-risk tickets with CS sign-off
  • Review backlog health & remaining debt
  • Document learnings for future sprints
  • Report outcomes to leadership with data
Two tracks — customer retention fixes and global delivery — run in parallel, not sequentially. I protect engineering focus not by cutting scope, but by reducing ambiguity. Clean requirements and tight handovers cut context-switching cost more than scope reductions do.

Case 02 · Global vs. Regional

A global feature that doesn't fit the U.S. market. Who wins?

The situation: A globally-built feature for Construction Wire is planned for rollout in North America. U.S. Sales and customers say the workflows and data don't match local reality. Global Product won't change it — citing roadmap commitments and scalability concerns. Commercial teams are pushing for adjustments to hit near-term revenue goals.
Misaligned Workflows Global Roadmap Friction Near-term Revenue Pressure
Case 02 · Diagnosis & Alignment

Separate opinion from evidence. Build a shared problem statement.

Step 1 · Evidence Gathering
Turn "it doesn't fit" into documented, specific gaps
Run structured interviews with 3–5 U.S. Sales reps and 2–3 customers. Map exact friction points: which workflow step breaks, what data is missing, what the workaround is. Quantify revenue exposure.
Step 2 · Global Product Bridge
Bring data and a proposal — not a complaint
Frame as a shared risk: "Here are 3 specific gaps blocking NA adoption. Which ones can we solve without derailing the roadmap?" Collaborative, not confrontational.
Step 3 · Classify the Feedback
Not all feedback is equal
Blockers Prevent adoption — must resolve pre-launch

Workarounds Painful but functional — document & log

Preferences Park until post-launch data is available
Step 4 · Commercial Alignment
Give Sales a message that's true and useful
Define what can be promised at launch vs. what's on the roadmap. Avoid overpromising unconfirmed features. Build enablement that works with the product as it exists today.
Assumption

At least one reachable Global Product counterpart is willing to engage. If not — building that relationship is day one priority, before any change request exists.

Case 02 · Launch Decision

Three outcomes. One clear decision process.

Outcome A
Delay & Fix
True blockers that can't be worked around → delay with a fixed date. Not "we're working on it" — a real date. Communicate proactively to Commercial.
Outcome B
Launch with Guardrails
Gaps are painful but non-blocking → launch to a controlled cohort (3–5 accounts). Document workarounds. Use real adoption data to build the case for Global roadmap changes.
Outcome C
Full Launch + Commit
Gaps are preference-level → launch fully. Lock a written roadmap commitment for NA-specific improvements and give Commercial a timeline for their conversations.
The decision isn't mine alone — but I own making it clear, fast, and documented. Strong opinions from multiple sides are a signal to move toward data, not toward consensus-seeking. Consensus-seeking in ambiguous situations produces delay, not alignment.

Case 03 · Product-Led Growth

Missing targets. Low engagement. Low conversion. Where does the problem actually live?

The situation: A product line is not hitting growth targets. Initial investigation reveals that both user engagement metrics and conversion rates are below expectations. The task is to diagnose the root cause and identify a clear, sequenced path to growth — not just optimize symptoms.
Below-target Conversion Low Engagement Realignment Needed

Diagnostic questions per stage
  • A
    Activation drop-off
    Where in onboarding do users stop? Is there a step that requires data they don't have yet?
  • R
    Retention signal
    Which users do retain? What feature did they use first? That's the aha moment — engineer onboarding toward it.
  • C
    Conversion blockers
    Are users getting real value before the ask? Is the value prop clear at the moment of conversion?
Assumption

Basic product analytics (event tracking) exists. If not — instrumenting the funnel is step zero. Without data, you're optimizing by intuition, which is expensive and usually wrong.

Case 03 · Realignment

Fix the floor before you build the ceiling.

Track 1 · Fix Activation
Reduce time-to-value
Identify the single action that predicts retention. Redesign onboarding to drive users there within session one. Remove every friction step that isn't strictly necessary. Test with a small cohort first.
Track 2 · Improve Retention
Reinforce the habit loop
Interview retained users — not churned ones. What do they use most, and when? Build lightweight triggers (notifications, digests, in-app nudges) that bring disengaged users back to the core value moment.
Track 3 · Conversion
Align the ask to the value moment
Don't optimize conversion in isolation. Fix activation and retention first — then test conversion levers with users already experiencing value. Premature conversion pushes erode trust fast.
Track 4 · Commercial Alignment
Sync Sales & CS on engagement signals
Share engagement data with CS to identify at-risk accounts early. Give Sales clear criteria for when a user is product-ready for an upsell. Prevent premature asks that show up in the next renewal cycle.
Growth isn't a single lever — it's a sequence. Activation rate as the leading indicator, retention as proof, conversion as the outcome. When every team agrees on that sequence, you stop having the wrong arguments.
